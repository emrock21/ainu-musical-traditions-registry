# Ainu Musical Traditions Registry

This repository preserves, in an immutable and decentralized format, the musical traditions of the Ainu — the Indigenous people of northern Japan (Hokkaidō) and Sakhalin. Ainu music is one of the most distinctive Indigenous sound systems in the North Pacific, combining vocal polyphony, ritual dance, string instruments, and breath-based resonance.

The registry documents major traditions such as **upopo** (group singing), **rimse** (ritual dance), **tonkori** (Sakhalin Ainu string instrument), **mukkuri** (jaw harp), **yukar** (epic chanting), and ceremonial songs dedicated to kamuy (spirits).

## Why Ainu Music Matters

Ainu musical traditions are:

- a **living archive of Indigenous memory**  
- a **ritual technology** for communicating with kamuy  
- a **communal practice** centered on women’s voices  
- a **sonic expression of cosmology**  
- a **survivor of cultural suppression**, preserved through resilience  

Upopo and rimse are among the few Indigenous musical forms in East Asia that survived both state assimilation and missionary pressure.

## Historical Suppression

During the Meiji period and early 20th century:

- Ainu language was banned in schools  
- musical gatherings were discouraged or prohibited  
- instruments like tonkori nearly disappeared  
- mukkuri playing was labeled “primitive”  
- ritual songs were suppressed as “pagan”  

Despite this, Ainu women preserved upopo and mukkuri in domestic spaces, ensuring the tradition survived.

Documenting these traditions on-chain is an act of **cultural vindication** and **sonic restitution**.

## Purpose of This Contract

The goal of this registry is to:

1. **Create a permanent, censorship-resistant record** of Ainu musical traditions.  
2. **Preserve Indigenous sound knowledge** that was nearly erased.  
3. **Document instruments, vocal styles, techniques, and symbolism** with precision.  
4. **Provide an immutable reference** for museums, researchers, and future generations.  
5. **Honor the Ainu communities who kept these traditions alive.**

The contract is intentionally:

- **ownerless** — no authority can alter or censor the registry  
- **payment-free** — no monetization, no barriers  
- **minimalistic and secure** — no external calls  
- **permanent** — entries become part of the cultural ledger  

## What Each Entry Records

- **traditionName** — upopo, rimse, tonkori, mukkuri, yukar  
- **instruments** — tonkori, mukkuri, percussion, voice  
- **vocalStyle** — call-and-response, chanting, polyphonic patterns  
- **techniques** — plucking, breath resonance, rhythmic stamping  
- **symbolism** — spiritual meaning, social function  
- **culturalContext** — ceremonies, gatherings, seasonal rituals
